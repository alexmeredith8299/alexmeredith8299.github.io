### Overview
Disclaimer: this advice is based on my personal experience[^1], so it is probably most applicable to people in similar fields (aerospace engineering) or at similar institutions (MIT), and who are US citizens or permanent residents. 

I'm focusing here on fellowships that provide stipend + tuition support for PhD students for multiple years. There are whole other categories of fellowships, like fellowships that provide internships/professional development[^2], prestigious fellowships for Americans to study abroad for a couple years after college[^3], and fellowships that provide a single year of funding for PhD students[^4].

Many fellowships are only open to people who either haven't started grad school, or who are still early-stage graduate students, so this advice will probably be most useful to undergraduate seniors and bachelor's degree holders applying to PhD programs, as well as first-year graduate students. However, some fellowships allow later-stage graduate students to apply, so don't lose hope!

### Fellowship information
This is a highly non-exhaustive list of fellowships which may be useful to apply to. I've done my best to list the duration of funding, approximate due date, and citizenship + graduate school stage requirements for each fellowship, but this information may be incorrect, incomplete, or outdated.

Some of these fellowships require internships or research stays at federally funded research centers, and some are only open to students at certain universities. Most are also only open to graduate students in certain fields of study -- but aerospace engineering students are eligible for all of these!

- [National Science Foundation Graduate Research Fellowship Program (NSF GRFP)](https://www.nsfgrfp.org/)
    - 3 years of tuition + stipend
    - Must be US citizen, US national, or permanent resident
    - Undergraduate seniors and bachelor's degree holders eligible
    - First-year graduate students eligible
    - Second-year graduate students eligible if didn't apply in first year of graduate school
    - Due in October (usually)
- [NASA Space Technology Graduate Research Opportunities (NSTGRO)](https://www.nasa.gov/nasa-space-technology-graduate-research-opportunities-nstgro/)
    - Up to 4 years of tuition + stipend
    - Must be US citizen, US national, or permanent resident
    - Eligibility criteria are complicated, but may be eligible if have completed < 3 years of graduate school
    - Due in November (usually)
- [Future Investigators in NASA Earth and Space Science and Technology (FINESST)](https://science.nasa.gov/researchers/solicitations/roses-2023/amendment-61-future-investigators-in-nasa-earth-and-space-science-and-technology-smds-graduate-student-research-proposals-final-text-released-proposals-due-february-6-2024/)
    - Up to 3 years of tuition + stipend, capped at $50k per year in total costs
    - No citizenship requirements!
    - Due in February (usually)
- [Hertz Fellowship](https://www.hertzfoundation.org/the-fellowship/eligibility-benefits/)
    - Up to 5 years of tuition + stipend
    - Must be US citizen or permanent resident
    - Undergraduate seniors and bachelor's degree holders eligible
    - First-year graduate students eligible
    - Due in October (usually)
- [GEM Fellowship](https://www.gemfellowship.org/gem-fellowship-program/)
    - Up to 5 years of tuition + stipend guaranteed through RAship/TAship (for PhD students)
    - Must be US citizen or permanent resident
    - Undergraduate seniors and bachelor's degree holders eligible
    - Master's students eligible
    - Due in November (usually)
- [Draper Scholars](https://www.draper.com/careers/scholar-program)
    - Up to 5 years of tuition + stipend
    - Must be US citizen
    - Due in February (usually)

### Why apply to fellowships?

- **Applying to fellowships forces you to think about research topics**. 

Many fellowship applications require research proposals. In my experience, writing a short research proposal as a prospective or first-year graduate student is a very useful exercise. My master's thesis project was actually pretty close to what I proposed in the fellowship applications I wrote during my first semester of grad school.

- **Fellowships make it easier to get into grad school**.

Fellowship awardees often have an easier time getting into grad school, even to the point of getting un-rejected after receiving a fellowship. Many schools would like to accept more PhD students, but can't afford to pay more students -- but if you bring 3-5 years of your own fellowship funding, you may suddenly become affordable and therefore admissable.

- **At some schools, fellowship awardees get extra pay**.

Most fellowships guarantee a minimum stipend, which may be higher than your school's stipend. At one conference, I met some NSTGRO awardees who earned $40k at a school in a low-cost-of-living area where most PhD students made around $20k. Some schools will also supplement fellowship stipends to reward students for bringing in fellowship money.

- **Fellowships guarantee funding**. 

Some schools/departments, including MIT AeroAstro, do not guarantee 5 years of tuition + stipend support for PhD students, so some students need to constantly seek out TAships, RAships, grants, and fellowships in order to stay funded. Having 3+ years of guaranteed fellowship funding provides some stress relief.

- **Fellowships offer flexibility**. 

Generally, the alternatives to fellowships are teaching assistantships (TAships) and research assistantships (RAships), which usually require around 20 hours/week of work providing teaching support or working on a specific research project. Fellowship awardees usually have more freedom than RAs to choose their own research topics, and have more time than TAs (and often RAs) to dedicate to thesis research. 

- **Some fellowships offer additional perks**. 

Common fellowship perks include mentorship, guaranteed internship placements, high-performance computing time, funding for lab equipment, funding for conference travel, and exclusive networking opportunities.

### General application advice

- **Emphasize past research experience in your personal statement**.

Remember that these are *research* fellowships. If you have past non-research internships or industry experience, it's great to mention that, but you should dedicate a lot more space to discussing your past and ongoing research experience. Open-ended capstone-style projects are also good to mention -- the more research-y, the better.

- **Highlight your publications and planned future publications in your personal statement**.

If you have any publications, even if you're like fifteenth author, you should strongly emphasize them. You can also mention planned future publications in a variety of ways. If you have any preprints or manuscripts under review, mention them. If your research is in an earlier stage, you can say things like "I'm currently preparing a manuscript", or "I expect to submit a paper to X journal/conference in the next Y months" (but don't lie). If you are the primary author, you should mention that explicitly as well.

- **Bold important phrases or sentences in your personal statement and research proposal**.

Reviewers skim. Any mentions of awards/honors/publications should be emphasized with bold text. If you look at examples of successful applications, you should get a sense of how to use bold text most effectively.

- **Help your recommenders help you**.

Most fellowships require multiple letters of recommendation (typically from professors or researchers with PhDs). When you ask people to write letters of recommendation for you, send them your personal statement + research proposal, and a document with
information on how to submit letters for each fellowship, as well as deadlines. You should also offer to meet with them. This will really help them write letters that accurately portray your goals and interests, and will help make sure they get the letters in on time.You should also feel free to email your recommenders before each due date to remind them to submit your letters.

- **Start early and apply widely**.

This is pretty obvious, but it's worth taking the time to polish your application materials and seek out feedback on your materials. Remember that feedback is a gift which will improve your application. Don't be embarrassed to show your application to other people. If you're already in grad school, ask your advisor to review your materials, and ask them to suggest fellowships to apply to.

- **Adapt your application materials to each fellowship**.

This is also pretty obvious. Most fellowships require some type of personal statement and research proposal, so it's tempting to submit the same materials everywhere with minimal changes, but tailoring your materials to each fellowship shows that you "understand the assignment" and will result in better reviews.

- **Learn from others' successful applications**.

Most successfully awarded applications look pretty similar and somewhat formulaic. Modelling your materials after successful applications ensures that you don't leave any important information out. If you'd like to see examples for the NSF GRFP or NSTGRO, feel free to [email me](mailto:ameredit@mit.edu) to ask for my application materials.

- **Learn from reviewer feedback**.

Certain fellowships allow you to apply multiple times, and provide reviewer feedback on each application. Reviewer feedback is incredibly helpful for understanding the strengths and weaknesses of your application. When I applied to NSTGRO, even though I was awarded a fellowship, I received feedback that my research proposal was too narrow in scope for a PhD -- this was definitely true and was helpful to hear early on. I also have a friend who applied to the NSF GRFP as an undergrad unsuccessfully, but took reviewer feedback to heart, reapplied two years later, and was awarded the fellowship.

### Advice on the NSF GRFP
Alex Lang has a [lot of useful advice](https://www.alexhunterlang.com/nsf-fellowship), including a spreadsheet with lots of successful applications from many different fields -- you should read at least 5 recent successful applications from similar fields to have an idea of what successful applications look like.

I basically agree with Alex's advice, but here are a few extra tips:
- **You should (probably) only apply as a first-year grad student if you didn't apply as an undergrad, and if you think your application would be competitive.**

The NSF GRFP only allows you to apply once as a grad student, in either your first or second year. If you applied unsuccessfully as an undergrad, unless you really feel you've addressed all the weaknesses of your application in the past year, I recommend waiting until your second year of grad school so that you have more time to gain research experience, improve your grades, hone your application materials, and generally show growth. However, in some situations, it might still make sense to apply during your first year of grad school -- like if your grad program doesn't include much research in the first year.

If you haven't previously applied to the NSF GRFP, it makes sense to apply in your first year of grad school if you have a strong undergraduate GPA and have done a decent amount of research before graduate school. Otherwise, wait a year so that you can submit a grad GPA and have more research experience. You can also ask your advisor about your specific situation if you're not sure how competitive your application will be.

- **Highlight any past funding or opportunities you've received from the NSF**.

I got this advice from my advisor when I applied. If your current research or any past research projects were funded (even in part) by the NSF, you should definitely mention that explicitly. I even mentioned in my application that I participated in a math outreach program in middle school which was funded by the NSF. 

- **Mention any past or planned future DEI work, service, or outreach in the broader impacts section.**

"Broader impacts" include the impact of your proposed research on the field, but also the impact of your research on society, and any outreach, service, or DEI work you plan to engage in. Think of broader impacts as sort of a grab bag of positive externalities of your research. You should definitely highlight DEI and outreach work here, and you can also mention participation in professional societies for underrepresented minorities and women (like NSBE, SHPE, SWE, etc.).
### Advice on NSTGRO
My former advisor on the NSTGRO project narrative:
>Intro (1 page)
>- Cite a NASA or National Academies guiding or strategic document that includes the technology you are proposing as a priority
>- Spend 1-2 paragraphs respectfully capturing the state of the art with cites (some of the cites might be your reviewers!)
>- Spend 1-2 paragraphs (politely, but firmly) capturing what is missing in the state of the art
>- State what parts of what is missing you will focus on
>
>Approach (1 page)
>- Restate the "research gap" you will focus on, but now break it into 3 steps (each step can have sub-parts)
>- Research Step 1: what tools will you use that exist? what tools will you need to develop? what collaborators would you like to engage with? (bonus points if at NASA center other than JPL)
>- Research Step 2: repeat same content structure as Step 1, but for the next part of the work
>- Research Step 3:  repeat same content structure as Step 1, but for the next part of the work
>- Closing paragraph that talks about open-sourcing tools developed and then moves into Analysis/Results section
>
>Analysis/Results (1 page)
>- For Research Step 1, once you apply your approach, what parameters will you vary and what are some expected/desired results if it all goes well? If it doesn't go well, what are your back-up options?
>- For Research Step 2, once you apply your approach, what parameters will you vary and what are some expected/desired results if it all goes well? If it doesn't go well, what are your back-up options?
>- For Research Step 3, once you apply your approach, what parameters will you vary and what are some expected/desired results if it all goes well? If it doesn't go well, what are your back-up options?
>
>Summary/Future Work
>- How will you document and communicate your results to the research community?
>- What journals would you publish in?
>- What conferences would you attend? 
>- What NASA centers would you give a seminar at?
>- How does your work fit into an extended effort to solve the "strategic document" problem you first mention? 
>- If you are successful, how will your work impact the science community? 
>- If you are successful, how will your work impact society (outreach and education of others along the way counts)?
>- Remember to discuss margin and a backup plan in case things don't go perfectly (data not available, facilities take long, procurement takes long, things go all virtual)"

My additional advice:
- **Everyone wants to collaborate with JPL, so maybe choose a different NASA center**.

This is also mentioned above, but it bears repeating. If location matters a lot to you, Ames, Goddard, and Langley are probably the next best bet after JPL.

- **Feedback from people at NASA is very helpful**.

I think that it probably helps if you already have people at NASA who want to work with you, and it definitely helps to have people at NASA look over your application and give advice on how to tweak your project narrative to best fit NASA's priorities. If you or your advisor have NASA collaborators or know people at any NASA center, I would definitely reach out to them before applying.

- **It's probably easiest to apply once you're already in grad school, unless you work at NASA**.

Although senior undergrads and bachelor's degree holders are eligible to apply, the length of the research statement and the amount of networking/NASA internal politics involved means that successful applicants typically have a compelling research plan and some kind of networking connection to NASA. NASA engineers and scientists who are applying to grad school, current grad students, and Pathways interns are all better-prepared than your average senior undergrad. However, there is no penalty on applying multiple times, so it may be worth applying even if you don't fall into these categories.

### Advice on Draper Scholars

Only about half of Draper Scholars apply "cold" -- the other half have existing connections at Draper. If you or your advisor knows someone at Draper who can sort of sponsor your application by agreeing to work with you and suggesting a project that Draper is highly interested in, that is definitely the best way to apply. If you are a current Draper employee, you should be well-equipped to do this.

Draper Scholars funding can be combined with federal fellowships, so it can be a great solution for NSF, NSTGRO, or NDSEG
